Turtles You Can in Sri Lanka


Sri Lanka's biodiversity includes turtles, and the nation is renowned for its abundant turtle population and conservation efforts. Five different species of sea turtles may be found in Sri Lanka, and they are divided into groups based on how they nest and how they look. These species are all either endangered or vulnerable due to human activities such as overfishing, pollution and habitat destruction. Therefore it is crucial to protect these species to maintain a healthy marine ecosystem.



Green Sea Turtle


This huge sea turtle has an olive or brown heart-shaped shell and was given its name after the color of its fat, which was once used to make turtle soup. Adult males are smaller than females, and they don't have the hook-shaped jaws like other turtles do. Green turtles are widespread in tropical areas and are frequently seen near oceanic islands with broad sand beaches. Every nest contains between 100 and 170 eggs, and nesting takes place all year round. After an incubation period of roughly two months, the hatchlings emerge.



Loggerhead Turtle


The Loggerhead turtle is a reddish brown turtle with a huge head that grows to 1.2 meters in length. They also inhabit lagoons, bays, and estuaries and eat crustaceans and mollusks (their strong jaws have evolved to crush the shells of these animals). These marine turtles, which are the rarest in Sri Lanka, are reported to lay their eggs on the country's southern and southern-eastern beaches between September and March. In a single clutch, they can lay up to 175 eggs, and the hatchlings appear two months later.



Hawksbill Turtle


The Hawksbill turtle measures around 1 m in length and has an olive-green shell. The short, protruding, forward-facing jaw that resembles a bird's beak gives this creature its name. They are typically considered to just eat sea sponges, yet it has been shown that they also eat algae, coral, and shellfish. They are frequently found in reefs, bays, estuaries, and lagoons. These turtles land to lay clutches of up to 175 eggs, which hatch in around 2 months. Fortunately for them, the Hawksbill's flesh has been proven to be deadly, and eating its meat has been linked to multiple fatalities.
